Digital platforms often subcontract self-employed workers. We develop an equilibrium labor market model to quantify Spain's 2021 Riders’ Law, which sanctions platforms for hiring self-employed couriers in the food delivery sector. Couriers in the model trade-off flexibility and employability as subcontracted against higher wages as employees. Counterfactual simulations show that, absent sanctions, one-fifth of employees would instead be self-employed. However, sanctions reduce welfare by lowering average hourly wages in the sector by 7 percentage points. We find that redistributing sanction revenue as lump-sum transfers to riders would offset most of these welfare losses.
